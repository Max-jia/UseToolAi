## A Note Before We Start

**The rule: AI should help you think, not think for you.**

Every professor knows AI exists. Most have updated their policies. Here's what gets you in trouble: copy-pasting AI output and submitting it as your own work. Here's what doesn't: using AI to brainstorm, research, polish, and explain — then writing the final submission yourself.

Use these tools like you'd use a tutor, a writing center consultant, or a study group. Not like a ghostwriter.

## 1. Perplexity — Your Research Librarian

**The pitch:** Perplexity doesn't just answer questions — it searches the web, reads the sources, and cites them. Every claim comes with a clickable reference. This is the tool professors wish students would use.

**What it does well:**
- Real citations — every answer links to its source. You can actually verify the information.
- Pro Search mode (5 free/day) uses reasoning models for multi-step research — "compare the economic policies of X and Y, citing primary sources"
- Collections let you organize research by class or paper topic
- Upload PDFs to extract and cite specific passages
- Free tier's regular search is unlimited

**Where it falls short:**
- Not a writing tool — it finds information, it won't write your paper (which is the point)
- 5 Pro searches/day — use them for your most important research questions
- Regular (non-Pro) searches are less thorough for complex topics

**Price:** Free (5 Pro searches/day) / Pro $20/mo (50% student discount: $10/mo)

**How to use it right:** Start every research paper with Perplexity. Use it to find sources, understand the debate around a topic, and identify key authors. Then read the actual sources. Then write your paper.

**How to get in trouble:** Asking it "write my paper on X" and submitting the output. Don't do this.

## 2. Grammarly — Your Writing TA

**The pitch:** Grammarly doesn't write for you. It catches what you missed — grammar errors, awkward phrasing, unclear sentences. It's the equivalent of having a writing center tutor read your draft before submission.

**What it does well:**
- Grammar, spelling, punctuation — catches errors you've read past 10 times
- Tone detection — tells you if your academic writing sounds too casual or your email sounds accidentally aggressive
- Clarity suggestions — flags confusing sentences and suggests rewrites
- Works everywhere: browser, Word, Google Docs, desktop
- Free tier covers all core editing features

**Where it falls short:**
- Premium features (full-sentence rewrites, plagiarism checker) cost $12/mo
- English only — no use for papers in other languages
- Suggestions are sometimes wrong — you still need to use your judgment

**Price:** Free / Premium $12/mo

**How to use it right:** Write your draft first. Then run Grammarly. Review each suggestion and decide whether to accept it. You're the writer; Grammarly is the proofreader.

**How to get in trouble:** You won't. Using Grammarly is universally accepted — most universities explicitly allow it.

## 3. ChatGPT — Your Study Partner

**The pitch:** ChatGPT is the most versatile AI tool available. For students, the key is using it as an explainer and brainstorming partner — not an essay mill.

**What it does well:**
- Explain concepts at any level — "explain CRISPR to me like I'm a high school student" or "explain it like I'm a PhD candidate"
- Generate practice questions — "give me 10 practice problems on integration by parts with solutions"
- Brainstorm essay outlines — not writing the essay, but helping you structure your thoughts
- GPT Image 1.5 generates diagrams and visual explanations
- Free tier is generous and unlimited

**Where it falls short:**
- Hallucinates facts confidently — never cite ChatGPT as a source
- Free tier has ads (introduced Feb 2026 in US)
- Writing can sound generic unless you prompt carefully

**Price:** Free / Plus $20/mo

**How to use it right:** Use ChatGPT to understand concepts you're struggling with, brainstorm paper outlines, and generate practice problems. Always verify facts against real sources. Write your own work.

**How to get in trouble:** Copy-pasting ChatGPT output as your own essay. Professors can tell. AI detection tools exist. More importantly, you're cheating yourself out of learning.

## 4. Jenni AI — Built for Academic Writing

**The pitch:** Jenni AI is the only tool on this list purpose-built for academic writing. It helps with structure, citations, and overcoming writer's block — while keeping you in the driver's seat.

**What it does well:**
- Auto-generates citations in APA, MLA, Chicago, Harvard, and more
- Built-in plagiarism checker
- AI autocomplete: when you're stuck mid-sentence, Jenni suggests the next phrase — you decide whether to accept
- PDF upload for extracting quotes and references from research papers
- Designed with academic integrity as a feature, not an afterthought

**Where it falls short:**
- Free tier: 200 words/day — enough for daily writing sessions, tight for marathon paper-writing
- Only useful for academic work — overkill for casual writing
- Learning curve — more complex interface than ChatGPT

**Price:** Free (200 words/day) / Unlimited $12/mo

**How to use it right:** Jenni is designed for exactly how students should use AI — augmentation, not replacement. Use it for citations, structure suggestions, and getting unstuck. Write the substantive content yourself.

**How to get in trouble:** You're unlikely to — Jenni's design philosophy is academic integrity. But as always, the final work should be yours.

## 5. Claude — Your Deep Reading Assistant

**The pitch:** Claude's 1M token context window means you can upload an entire textbook, a semester's worth of readings, or a 50-page research paper — and ask questions across all of it.

**What it does well:**
- 1M token context — upload everything and ask "what are the common themes across these five papers?"
- Exceptional at summarizing long, complex documents without losing nuance
- Writing that sounds natural — useful for understanding how to phrase complex ideas
- No training on user data — your uploaded readings stay private

**Where it falls short:**
- Free tier: ~20-30 messages/day before switching to a smaller model
- No web search or image generation — text only
- No access from China and some restricted regions

**Price:** Free / Pro $20/mo

**How to use it right:** Upload your assigned readings. Ask Claude to summarize, identify key arguments, and explain difficult passages. Use this to prepare for seminars and paper writing. The understanding should be yours; Claude is the accelerator.

**How to get in trouble:** Using Claude to understand readings is fine — that's what office hours are for. Uploading a prompt that says "write my paper based on these readings" is not.

## 6. Otter — Never Miss a Lecture Point Again

**The pitch:** Otter transcribes lectures in real time. You stay engaged with what the professor is saying instead of frantically typing every word. Later, search the transcript for anything you vaguely remember.

**What it does well:**
- Real-time transcription — words appear as they're spoken
- Highlight and tag key moments live
- Speaker identification — knows when the professor is talking vs a classmate's question
- Searchable archive — "what did Professor Chen say about mitochondria in week 4?"
- 300 free minutes/month, 30 minutes per session

**Where it falls short:**
- 300 minutes/month — heavy lecture schedules will exceed this
- 30-minute cap per session on free tier — long lectures get cut off
- Requires permission in some classrooms — check your professor's recording policy

**Price:** Free (300 mins/month) / Pro $8/seat/mo

**How to use it right:** Otter records what was said. You still need to do the thinking — review transcripts, identify what's important, synthesize into study notes. The transcript is raw material, not finished understanding.

**How to get in trouble:** Recording lectures without permission. Most universities have policies — check before you record.

## 7. Gamma — Presentations Without the 3 AM Panic

**The pitch:** Gamma generates presentations from a prompt. But the smart student move is using it to structure your thoughts and create a visual framework — then filling in the actual content yourself.

**What it does well:**
- Generate presentation structure from a topic — "10-slide presentation on the causes of WWI"
- Clean, modern designs — doesn't look like a template
- Export to PowerPoint for in-class presentations
- Built-in analytics show you how viewers engaged
- 400 free AI credits (roughly 10 presentations)

**Where it falls short:**
- AI-generated content is surface-level — you need to add depth and your own arguments
- Free tier presentations are public
- 50 lifetime credits for AI features (separate from the 400 AI credits)

**Price:** Free (400 AI credits) / Plus $8/mo (50% student discount available)

**How to use it right:** Generate the structure and visual framework. Replace the AI-generated bullet points with your own research and analysis. The presentation should communicate your thinking, not Gamma's.

**How to get in trouble:** Presenting AI-generated content as your own analysis. If you don't add your own thinking, it shows — and your grade will reflect it.

## FAQ

### Q: Will my professor know I used AI?

**A:** If you copy-paste AI output, yes. AI-generated text has detectable patterns — consistent tone, lack of personal voice, generic arguments. AI detection tools exist (Turnitin, GPTZero). But using AI for research, brainstorming, and editing — then writing the final work yourself — is both undetectable and academically legitimate. The best way to not get caught: actually do the work.

### Q: Can I use the free tiers for an entire semester?

**A:** Mostly yes. Perplexity's 5 Pro searches/day + unlimited regular searches cover research. Grammarly free covers editing. ChatGPT free covers everything else. The only bottleneck is Jenni AI (200 words/day) if you're writing a thesis. Otter's 300 minutes/month may run out during heavy lecture weeks. But for a typical semester, the free tiers are sufficient.

### Q: What's the one tool I should start with?

**A:** Perplexity. Research is the foundation of academic work, and Perplexity's citation-backed approach teaches good habits — verify sources, read primary material, build arguments on evidence. It's the tool that makes you a better student, not just a more efficient one.
