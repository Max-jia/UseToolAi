AI writing has a voice. You know it when you read it. "In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape…" "It is important to note that…" "Furthermore, it is worth mentioning…"

Nobody talks like this. Nobody wants to read it either.

Five techniques to make AI-generated text sound like a human wrote it — and stop getting flagged by AI detectors.

---

## The Short Version

1. **Give the AI a voice, not just a prompt** — Feed it writing samples and tell it to mimic the style
2. **Ban the AI words** — Explicitly tell the AI which words and phrases to avoid
3. **Chunk it** — Don't ask for a full article. Ask for sections, then stitch them together
4. **Edit like a human editor** — AI output is a first draft. Treat it like one
5. **Run it through an AI detector — then fix what gets flagged**

---

## 1. Give the AI a Voice (Not Just a Prompt)

The #1 mistake: typing "write a blog post about X" and accepting the output.

AI defaults to a generic, corporate voice because it's trained on the average of the entire internet. You need to override that default.

### How to do it:

**Step 1:** Find 3 pieces of your own writing — blog posts, emails, social media posts — anything in your natural voice.

**Step 2:** Paste them into the AI with this prompt:

```
Here are three examples of my writing style. Analyze the patterns:
- Sentence length and structure
- Vocabulary level
- Use of humor, sarcasm, or casual language
- How I open and close pieces
- Any signature phrases or habits

[Paste your 3 writing samples here]
```

**Step 3:** The AI will describe your style back to you. Save that description.

**Step 4:** Now when you want it to write something, start with:

```
Write in my style based on this description:
[Paste the style description from Step 3]

Topic: [your topic]
```

### Why this works:

You're not asking the AI to write like "a blogger." You're asking it to write like *you*. The difference in output is dramatic — shorter sentences, your vocabulary patterns, your rhythms.

If you don't have writing samples (or don't want to use your own voice), pick a writer you admire. Feed the AI 3 articles by that writer. It will approximate their style.

## 2. Ban the AI Words (Explicitly)

AI models overuse certain words and phrases. ChatGPT is notorious for "delve," "unleash," "tapestry," "landscape," and "it's worth noting that." Claude is better but still has tells.

### The fix: a banned words list.

Add this to every writing prompt:

```
NEVER use these words or phrases:
delve, unleash, tapestry, landscape, realm, crucial, furthermore,
moreover, it is worth noting that, in today's world, game-changer,
unlock your potential, elevate your, take it to the next level,
in the rapidly evolving, cutting-edge, revolutionary, seamless

Write like a human, not a corporate press release.
```

### Why this works:

AI doesn't have taste — it has statistical patterns. These words appear frequently in AI training data paired with "professional writing." By explicitly banning them, you force the model to reach for less common, more natural alternatives.

### Bonus: the "read it out loud" test.

After generating, read the text aloud. If you stumble over a sentence or it sounds like a press release, rewrite it. AI is allergic to short, punchy sentences. Humans love them. Add some.

## 3. Chunk It — Never Ask for a Full Article

AI loses the plot on long-form content. The introduction is robotic. Paragraph three repeats paragraph one. By the conclusion, it's summarizing things it never actually said.

### The fix: build articles piece by piece.

**Step 1:** Generate the outline first.

```
Give me a 5-section outline for a blog post about [topic].
Each section should have a clear point, not a vague heading.
```

**Step 2:** Write each section separately.

```
Write Section 1 of this outline: [paste the section title and bullet points].
Use my style. Keep it to 3 short paragraphs. End with a transition to Section 2.
```

**Step 3:** Generate transitions between sections manually or with a separate prompt.

**Step 4:** Write the intro *last*. Now you know what the article actually says, so the intro will be accurate and specific rather than generic and vague.

### Why this works:

AI performs better on focused, single-topic tasks than on sprawling ones. Each section gets full attention. The transitions feel natural because you're writing them to connect actual content, not hypothetical content the AI imagined in paragraph one.

## 4. Edit Like a Human Editor (Not a Proofreader)

The difference between AI slop and good AI-assisted writing is the editing pass. Most people fix typos and call it done. That's proofreading, not editing.

### The human editor's checklist:

- [ ] **Cut the first paragraph.** AI intros are always generic. The real opening is usually paragraph two or three.
- [ ] **Break long sentences.** AI loves compound sentences. Split anything over 25 words into two sentences.
- [ ] **Add a personal anecdote.** AI can't tell stories from your life. Add one sentence about your experience. "When I first tried this…" or "A client once asked me…"
- [ ] **Add a hot take.** AI is balanced to a fault. Add one strong opinion. "Most SEO advice is garbage." The AI won't do this. You should.
- [ ] **Remove hedging.** Delete "generally," "typically," "often," "it could be argued that." State things plainly.
- [ ] **Vary sentence rhythm.** Short. Punchy. Then a slightly longer one that flows naturally and gives the reader room to breathe. Then short again.

### Time budget:

For a 1,000-word post: 5 minutes generating with AI. 15 minutes editing. The editing is where the writing actually happens.

## 5. Run It Through an AI Detector — Then Fix It

AI detectors are not reliable enough to make academic decisions. But they're reliable enough to tell you when your writing sounds robotic. Use them as a diagnostic, not a judge.

### The process:

1. **Run your draft through GPTZero or Originality.ai.** If it scores below 50% "human," your editing pass wasn't thorough enough.
2. **Identify flagged sentences.** The detector will highlight specific passages.
3. **Rewrite those sentences from scratch.** Don't just swap synonyms — restructure the sentence entirely. Change the word order. Add a contraction. Make it conversational.
4. **Re-test.** Aim for 70%+ "human" score. 100% is not necessary — some human writing flags as AI because it's well-structured.

### A better test than AI detectors:

**Send the draft to a friend.** Ask: "Does this sound like me, or like ChatGPT?" If they hesitate, edit more.

## The Complete Prompt Template

Copy this. Fill in the brackets. Use it for every piece of AI-assisted writing.

```
You are writing in my style. Here is my style description:
[Paste style analysis from Section 1]

Topic: [your topic]
Target audience: [who are you writing for?]
Word count: [approximate]

Structure:
- Open with [specific hook — not a generic intro]
- Cover [point 1], [point 2], [point 3]
- Close with [specific takeaway or call to action]

Rules:
- NEVER use these words: delve, unleash, tapestry, landscape, realm, crucial, furthermore, moreover, it is worth noting that, game-changer, revolutionary, seamless, cutting-edge
- Maximum 3 sentences per paragraph
- Use contractions (don't, can't, it's)
- Write at a [grade level] reading level
- Include one specific example or data point per section
- Do not write a generic introduction or conclusion
```

## FAQ

### Q: Will Google penalize AI-written content?

**A:** No — if it's good. Google's official position: AI-generated content is fine as long as it's helpful, original, and created for people. The method doesn't matter. The quality does. Heavily edited AI content is indistinguishable from human-written content because a human did the final pass.

### Q: How do I know if my content sounds like AI?

**A:** Three tests. Read it out loud — does it sound like you talking? Send it to a friend — does it sound like you or a robot? Run it through an AI detector — not as a verdict, but as a diagnostic. If two out of three fail, edit more.

### Q: Which AI tool produces the most natural writing?

**A:** Claude. It's consistently rated the least "AI-sounding" of all major assistants. ChatGPT is more versatile (images, voice, web search) but requires more careful prompting to avoid generic output. For writing that needs to sound human, Claude is the better tool.
