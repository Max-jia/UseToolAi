Sudowrite is the fiction writer's AI companion — and it's in a category of its own. While ChatGPT and Claude can write prose, they weren't built for novel-length narrative. Sudowrite's Story Engine understands that a character's voice in Chapter 3 should match Chapter 30, that pacing needs to vary across acts, and that "show, don't tell" applies differently in thrillers versus literary fiction. The tool is built by fiction authors for fiction authors, and that domain focus shows in every feature.

The Story Engine workflow is genuinely impressive: you provide a synopsis and character sketches, and Sudowrite generates a chapter-by-chapter outline, then expands each into full narrative prose — all while maintaining character consistency across 100K+ words. The first draft won't be publishable (no AI-generated novel has won a major literary prize yet), but it provides a solid foundation that cuts weeks off the drafting process. The prose polishing tools let you rewrite passages in different authorial styles, and the revision toolkit catches issues like repetitive sentence structure, flat dialogue, and pacing lulls that general-purpose AI completely misses.

Sudowrite is controversial in literary circles, and the ethical debate is real. Some authors use it as a co-writer, others as a brainstorming partner, and some reject it entirely. The tool works best as an accelerator for authors who already know their craft — it won't turn a non-writer into a novelist any more than a better camera turns someone into a cinematographer. At $19-44/month, it's priced comparably to Scrivener or ProWritingAid — reasonable for serious writers, expensive for dabblers. For fiction authors willing to engage with AI as a creative tool rather than a replacement, Sudowrite is unmatched.
