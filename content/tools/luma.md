Luma AI's Ray 3.2 model is built for professionals who will actually notice the difference between 8-bit and 16-bit color depth — and whose careers depend on that difference. Native HDR output and 16-bit EXR export aren't marketing bullet points; they're table stakes for integration into professional VFX pipelines where color grading happens in DaVinci Resolve and compositing in Nuke. If your videos end up on TikTok, you don't need this. If they end up in a feature film, a commercial spot, or a broadcast production, you absolutely do. Luma is one of the only AI video tools that colorists and VFX supervisors can take seriously.

The 8-face simultaneous tracking is the feature that separates Luma from Runway and Pika for multi-character work. Most AI video tools lose track of faces when more than 2-3 people appear in a scene — facial identity blurs, features drift, and consistency collapses. Luma maintains identity consistency across up to 8 faces simultaneously, which matters enormously for dialogue scenes, crowd shots, and any narrative content with an ensemble cast. The 16-keyframe system gives directors and animators fine-grained motion control that text-prompt-only tools simply can't match, making it possible to specify exactly how a subject moves through a scene rather than hoping the AI guesses correctly.

The pricing math is complex by design. Free tier gives 30 credits (~10 videos) — barely enough to evaluate the tool properly. Standard at $9.99/month (120 credits) is the entry point for hobbyists. The real jump is Plus at $30/month for 10,000 credits — clearly the intended sweet spot for regular creators. API pricing starts at $0.15 per 5-second 540p clip and scales to $1.20 for 1080p, making it viable for automated video pipelines. One frustration: different resolutions and durations consume wildly different credit amounts, making cost prediction difficult without a spreadsheet. If you're a social media creator making TikToks, InVideo or Pika will serve you better and cheaper with friendlier interfaces. If you're a filmmaker, VFX artist, or studio building automated video production pipelines, Luma is the only serious choice in the AI video space.
