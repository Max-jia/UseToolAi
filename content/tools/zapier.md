Zapier is the 800-pound gorilla of no-code automation. With 7,000+ integrations and billions of tasks processed monthly, it's the default choice for connecting SaaS tools — and for good reason. The platform is mature, reliable, and comprehensive in ways that younger competitors (Make, n8n, Pipedream) are still catching up to. Need to add new Typeform responses to a Google Sheet, then send a Slack notification, then create a HubSpot contact? Zapier handles this in minutes, no code required.

The 2025 AI-powered Zap builder was a significant improvement. Instead of manually selecting triggers, actions, and field mappings, you can now describe what you want in plain English ("when someone fills out my contact form, add them to my newsletter and send me a text"), and Zapier builds the workflow. It's not magic — complex Zaps still need manual tweaking — but it dramatically lowers the barrier for non-technical users who find the trigger-action paradigm unintuitive. Tables and Interfaces (2024-2025 additions) signal Zapier's ambition to move beyond "automation glue" into lightweight internal tool building, competing with the low end of Airtable and Retool.

The biggest drawback is pricing at scale. Zapier's task-based model means costs grow linearly with usage. A Zap that processes 10,000 rows of spreadsheet data uses 10,000 tasks. At Professional ($49/month for 1,000 tasks), you'll blow through your allowance in one heavy workflow. Make (formerly Integromat) offers significantly better value for high-volume users with operations-based pricing that's often 3-5x cheaper. For teams doing moderate automation (1,000-5,000 tasks/month), Zapier's maturity and integration breadth justify the premium. For high-volume scenarios, Make or Pipedream offer better economics.
